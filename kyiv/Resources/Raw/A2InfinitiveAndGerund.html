Use gerund

1. When the verb is the subject of a sentence.

  • Swimming in the ocean has been Sharon’s passion since she was five years old.

2. After a preposition.

  • She is afraid of flying.

3. After some verbs.

  • I like cooking.
  • She recommended visiting this museum.

Common verbs followed by a gerund

Some common verbs that are followed by gerund are: avoid, enjoy, finish, hate, keep, like, love, prefer, recommend, spend time, stop, don’t mind, suggest, etc.

Negative gerund

The negative form of the gerund is not + -ing.

  • He enjoys not having to wake up early at weekends.

Use infinitive without to

1. After the auxiliary verb do, does, did in negative sentences and questions.

  • She didn’t say anything.
  • Does Jack work with you?
  • I don’t believe you.

2. After modal verbs (will, can, must, should, might, may, etc.)

  • You should come with us.
  • I can’t play football.

Use to + infinitive

1. After adjectives.

  • It’s important to arrive early at the station.

2. To express a reason or purpose (why).

  • I went to London to visit some family.
  • I need time to study for the exam.

3. After question words.

  • I don’t know what to eat.
  • I want to learn how to play the guitar.

4. After some verbs.

  • Don’t forget to call me.
  • She seems to be distracted.

Common verbs followed by to + infinitive

Some common verbs that are followed by to infinitive are: ask (someone), decide, forget, help, hope, remember, seem, try, want, learn, need, offer, plan, promise, would like, would love, would hate, would prefer, etc.

The negative form of to + infinitive

The negative form of to + infinitive is not to + infinitive.

  • She decided not to enter the competition.
